Kinematics

Definitions

v = Δx / Δt
Average velocity
v = velocity (ms⁻¹), Δx = displacement (m), Δt = time interval (s)
a = Δv / Δt
Acceleration
a = acceleration (ms⁻²), Δv = change in velocity (ms⁻¹)
speed = distance / time
Average speed (scalar)
speed has no direction; velocity does

SUVAT — Equations of Motion (uniform acceleration only)

v = u + at
No displacement
u = initial velocity, v = final velocity, a = acceleration, t = time
s = ½(u + v)t
No acceleration
s = displacement (m)
s = ut + ½at²
No final velocity
Use when v is unknown
v² = u² + 2as
No time
Use when t is unknown
s = vt − ½at²
No initial velocity
Less commonly tested but valid

Projectile Motion

Horizontal: x = u cosθ × t
Horizontal displacement
No horizontal acceleration (ignoring air resistance)
Vertical: y = u sinθ × t − ½gt²
Vertical displacement
g = 9.81 ms⁻² downward
vₓ = u cosθ (constant)
Horizontal velocity
Unchanged throughout flight
vᵧ = u sinθ − gt
Vertical velocity
Decreases going up, increases coming down
At max height: vᵧ = 0
Peak of trajectory
Use v² = u² + 2as with v = 0 to find max height

Graphs of Motion

gradient of displacement–time = velocity
s–t graph
Curved line = changing velocity (acceleration); straight = constant velocity
gradient of velocity–time = acceleration
v–t graph
Area under v–t graph = displacement
gradient of acceleration–time = rate of change of acceleration
a–t graph
Area under a–t graph = change in velocity

Dynamics

Newton's Laws

Newton's 1st Law
Inertia
An object remains at rest or in uniform motion unless acted upon by a resultant force
F = ma
Newton's 2nd Law
F = resultant force (N), m = mass (kg), a = acceleration (ms⁻²)
Newton's 3rd Law
Action-reaction pairs
For every action there is an equal and opposite reaction. Forces act on DIFFERENT objects.

Momentum & Impulse

p = mv
Momentum
p = momentum (kg ms⁻¹), m = mass (kg), v = velocity (ms⁻¹)
F = Δp / Δt
Force = rate of change of momentum
Links Newton's 2nd law to momentum
Impulse = FΔt = Δp
Impulse
Units: N s or kg ms⁻¹ (same thing); area under F–t graph
Impulse = mv − mu
Impulse from velocities
Change in momentum

Conservation of Momentum

m₁u₁ + m₂u₂ = m₁v₁ + m₂v₂
Collision — conservation of momentum
Total momentum before = total momentum after (closed system, no external forces)
Elastic: KE conserved. Inelastic: KE not conserved.
Types of collision
In perfectly inelastic collisions, objects stick together: m₁u₁ + m₂u₂ = (m₁+m₂)v

Forces, Density & Pressure

Forces

W = mg
Weight
W = weight (N), m = mass (kg), g = 9.81 ms⁻²
F_friction = μN
Friction
μ = coefficient of friction, N = normal reaction force (N)
Centre of gravity
Definition
The single point where the entire weight of an object appears to act

Moments & Torque

Moment = F × d
Turning moment (torque)
F = force (N), d = perpendicular distance from pivot (m). Unit: Nm
Σ clockwise moments = Σ anticlockwise moments
Principle of moments (equilibrium)
Applies when object is in rotational equilibrium
Couple = F × d (two equal, opposite, parallel forces)
Couple / torque of a couple
d = perpendicular distance between the two forces. Produces rotation, no translation.

Equilibrium Conditions

ΣF = 0 (resultant force = 0)
Translational equilibrium
Sum of all forces in any direction = 0
Σmoments = 0 (resultant moment = 0)
Rotational equilibrium
Both conditions needed for full equilibrium

Density & Pressure

ρ = m / V
Density
ρ = density (kg m⁻³), m = mass (kg), V = volume (m³)
p = F / A
Pressure
p = pressure (Pa = N m⁻²), F = force (N), A = area (m²)
p = ρgh
Pressure in a fluid
h = depth below surface (m), g = 9.81 ms⁻², ρ = fluid density
Upthrust = ρVg
Archimedes' principle
V = volume of fluid displaced (m³); upthrust = weight of fluid displaced

Work, Energy & Power

Work Done

W = Fs cosθ
Work done
W = work (J), F = force (N), s = displacement (m), θ = angle between F and s
W = area under F–s graph
Work from graph
Useful for variable forces

Energy

KE = ½mv²
Kinetic energy
KE in joules (J), m = mass (kg), v = speed (ms⁻¹)
ΔKE = ½mv² − ½mu²
Change in kinetic energy
Equals work done by resultant force
GPE = mgh
Gravitational potential energy
h = height gained (m), g = 9.81 ms⁻²
ΔGPE = mgΔh
Change in GPE
Δh = change in height
KE gained = GPE lost (no friction)
Conservation of mechanical energy
Total mechanical energy conserved when no resistive forces act

Power & Efficiency

P = W / t
Power
P = power (W), W = work done (J), t = time (s)
P = Fv
Power of moving object
v = velocity (ms⁻¹); useful for constant-speed problems
efficiency = P_useful / P_input
Efficiency (as decimal)
Multiply × 100 for percentage
efficiency = W_useful / W_input
Efficiency (from work/energy)
Always ≤ 1 (or ≤ 100%)

Deformation of Solids

Hooke's Law & Springs

F = kx
Hooke's Law
F = force (N), k = spring constant (N m⁻¹), x = extension (m). Valid up to limit of proportionality.
E_spring = ½Fx = ½kx²
Elastic potential energy stored
Area under F–x graph. Valid only in elastic region.
Springs in series: 1/k_eff = 1/k₁ + 1/k₂
Combined spring constant (series)
Extension is shared; same force in each spring
Springs in parallel: k_eff = k₁ + k₂
Combined spring constant (parallel)
Same extension; force is shared

Stress, Strain & Young's Modulus

stress σ = F / A
Tensile stress
σ = stress (Pa), F = force (N), A = cross-sectional area (m²)
strain ε = x / L₀
Tensile strain
ε = strain (no units), x = extension (m), L₀ = original length (m)
E = σ / ε = (FL₀) / (Ax)
Young's modulus
E in pascals (Pa). Gradient of linear part of stress–strain graph.
Energy/volume = ½σε
Elastic strain energy density
Area under stress–strain graph (elastic region)

Material Behaviour (Key Terms)

Elastic — returns to original shape on removing force
Elastic deformation
Loading and unloading curves coincide. No permanent deformation.
Plastic — permanent deformation after force removed
Plastic deformation
Occurs beyond elastic limit. Loading and unloading curves differ.
Limit of proportionality
F ∝ x no longer holds beyond this point
Hooke's law breaks down
Elastic limit
Beyond here deformation is permanent
Just above limit of proportionality
Yield point
Large extension with little increase in force
Occurs in ductile materials (e.g. steel)
UTS — ultimate tensile strength
Maximum stress material can withstand
Peak of stress–strain curve

Waves

Wave Properties

v = fλ
Wave speed
v = speed (ms⁻¹), f = frequency (Hz), λ = wavelength (m)
T = 1 / f
Time period
T = period (s), f = frequency (Hz)
f = 1 / T
Frequency
Number of complete oscillations per second
I = P / A
Intensity
I = intensity (W m⁻²), P = power (W), A = area (m²)
I ∝ A² (amplitude squared)
Intensity–amplitude relationship
Double amplitude → quadruple intensity
I ∝ 1/r²
Inverse square law (point source)
r = distance from source; intensity decreases with square of distance

Transverse vs Longitudinal

Transverse — oscillation perpendicular to wave travel
Examples
Light, water waves, all EM waves. Can be polarised.
Longitudinal — oscillation parallel to wave travel
Examples
Sound waves, ultrasound. Cannot be polarised. Has compressions and rarefactions.

Electromagnetic Spectrum

c = fλ = 3.00 × 10⁸ ms⁻¹ (in vacuum)
All EM waves travel at speed of light in vacuum
Radio → Microwave → IR → Visible → UV → X-ray → Gamma (increasing f, decreasing λ)

Refraction

n₁ sinθ₁ = n₂ sinθ₂
Snell's Law
n = refractive index, θ = angle from normal
n = sin i / sin r
Snell's Law (air to medium)
i = angle of incidence, r = angle of refraction (both from normal)
n = c / v
Refractive index
c = speed in vacuum/air, v = speed in medium. n ≥ 1 always.
sin C = 1 / n
Critical angle (going from medium to air)
C = critical angle. TIR occurs when θ > C.
n = λ_air / λ_medium
Refractive index from wavelengths
Wavelength changes but frequency stays constant when entering a medium

Doppler Effect

f_obs = f_s × v / (v ± v_s)
Doppler formula (moving source)
Use − when source moves toward observer (higher f), + when moving away. v = wave speed, v_s = source speed.

Superposition

Principle of Superposition

Resultant displacement = sum of individual displacements
Superposition principle
Applies when two or more waves overlap at a point
Constructive: path diff = nλ → amplitude doubles
Constructive interference
n = 0, 1, 2, ... Waves arrive in phase.
Destructive: path diff = (n + ½)λ → amplitude = 0
Destructive interference
Waves arrive in antiphase (180° out of phase)

Young's Double Slit

λ = ax / D
Young's double slit formula
a = slit separation (m), x = fringe spacing (m), D = slit-to-screen distance (m)
x = λD / a
Fringe spacing
Larger D or smaller a → wider fringes

Diffraction Grating

d sinθ = nλ
Diffraction grating equation
d = grating spacing (m), θ = angle of diffraction, n = order (0,1,2...), λ = wavelength
d = 1 / N
Grating spacing from lines per metre
N = number of lines per metre. If given lines/mm, divide by 10⁶ to get d in metres.
max order n_max = d / λ (round down)
Maximum order visible
sinθ cannot exceed 1, so n ≤ d/λ

Stationary Waves

λ = 2L / n
Wavelength for nth harmonic (string/pipe open both ends)
L = length, n = harmonic number (1, 2, 3...)
f_n = n × v / (2L)
Frequency of nth harmonic
v = wave speed, L = length
f₁ = v / (2L) — fundamental (1st harmonic)
Fundamental frequency
Lowest possible frequency for resonance
Node — zero displacement always. Antinode — maximum displacement.
Key points on stationary wave
Adjacent node-antinode distance = λ/4. Adjacent node-node distance = λ/2.
Points between two nodes oscillate IN PHASE. Points on opposite sides of a node are in ANTIPHASE.
Phase in stationary waves
Unlike progressive waves, stationary waves do not transfer energy along the medium.

Electricity

Charge & Current

I = ΔQ / Δt
Current
I = current (A), Q = charge (C), t = time (s)
Q = It
Charge from current
Total charge transferred
I = nAvq
Drift velocity model
n = number density (m⁻³), A = cross-section (m²), v = drift velocity (ms⁻¹), q = charge per carrier (C)
e = 1.60 × 10⁻¹⁹ C
Elementary charge
Charge of one proton / magnitude of charge of one electron

Potential Difference & EMF

V = W / Q
Potential difference
V = p.d. (V), W = energy transferred (J), Q = charge (C)
ε = W / Q
EMF (electromotive force)
Energy supplied per unit charge by the source. Units: volts (V).
EMF — work done per unit charge by source. PD — work done per unit charge by circuit component.
EMF vs PD distinction
EMF is a source; PD is a drop across a load

Resistance & Ohm's Law

R = V / I
Resistance
R = resistance (Ω), V = voltage (V), I = current (A)
V = IR
Ohm's Law
V ∝ I at constant temperature for an ohmic conductor
R = ρL / A
Resistivity
ρ = resistivity (Ω m), L = length (m), A = cross-sectional area (m²)
Ohmic conductor: I ∝ V (constant R). Non-ohmic: R varies (e.g. filament lamp, diode, thermistor, LDR).
Ohmic vs non-ohmic
Filament lamp: resistance increases with temperature. Thermistor (NTC): resistance decreases with temperature. LDR: resistance decreases with light intensity.

Power & Energy in Circuits

P = IV
Electrical power
P = power (W)
P = I²R
Power (in terms of R)
Use when voltage unknown
P = V² / R
Power (in terms of V)
Use when current unknown
E = Pt = VIt = I²Rt
Energy dissipated
E = energy (J), t = time (s)
E = QV
Energy transferred by charge
Q = charge (C), V = p.d. (V)

DC Circuits

Kirchhoff's Laws

KCL: ΣI_in = ΣI_out at a junction
Kirchhoff's Current Law (1st Law)
Conservation of charge — charge does not accumulate at a junction
KVL: Σε = ΣIR around a loop
Kirchhoff's Voltage Law (2nd Law)
Conservation of energy — sum of EMFs = sum of p.d. drops around any closed loop

Series & Parallel Circuits

R_total = R₁ + R₂ + R₃ + ...
Resistors in series
Same current through each; voltages add up
1/R_total = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + ...
Resistors in parallel
Same voltage across each; currents add up. R_total always less than smallest R.
Two in parallel: R = R₁R₂ / (R₁+R₂)
Quick formula for two parallel resistors
Product over sum
V_series ∝ R (voltage divides proportionally to R)
Voltage in series
V₁/V₂ = R₁/R₂

Internal Resistance & EMF

ε = V + Ir
EMF with internal resistance
ε = EMF (V), V = terminal p.d. (V), I = current (A), r = internal resistance (Ω)
V = ε − Ir
Terminal potential difference
V decreases as I increases (when r > 0)
ε = I(R + r)
Total circuit equation
R = external resistance, r = internal resistance
gradient of V−I graph = −r, y-intercept = ε
Graphical method for ε and r
Plot terminal p.d. V against current I

Potential Divider

V_out = R₂ / (R₁ + R₂) × V_in
Potential divider output voltage
V_out taken across R₂. V_out ∝ R₂.
Thermistor in potential divider: as T↑, R_thermistor↓, V_thermistor↓
Temperature sensor application
Swap positions to get inverse relationship. Also applies to LDR with light.

Particle Physics

Atomic Structure

A = Z + N
Mass number
A = mass number, Z = proton number (atomic number), N = neutron number
Isotopes — same Z, different N (different number of neutrons)
Isotopes definition
Same chemical properties, different masses

Fundamental Particles

Proton: charge +e, mass ≈ 1u
Proton
e = 1.60 × 10⁻¹⁹ C, u = 1.66 × 10⁻²⁷ kg
Neutron: charge 0, mass ≈ 1u
Neutron
Slightly heavier than proton
Electron: charge −e, mass ≈ 1/1836 u
Electron
m_e = 9.11 × 10⁻³¹ kg

Radioactive Decay

α decay: A → (A−4), Z → (Z−2), emits ⁴₂He
Alpha decay
Stops in a few cm of air. Blocked by paper. High ionising power.
β⁻ decay: neutron → proton + electron + antineutrino. Z → (Z+1), A unchanged.
Beta-minus decay
Blocked by a few mm aluminium. Moderate ionising power.
β⁺ decay: proton → neutron + positron + neutrino. Z → (Z−1), A unchanged.
Beta-plus decay
Positron is antiparticle of electron
γ decay: no change in A or Z. Nucleus releases energy as gamma photon.
Gamma decay
Blocked by thick lead or concrete. Lowest ionising power. Highly penetrating.

Radioactive Decay Equations

A = λN
Activity
A = activity (Bq), λ = decay constant (s⁻¹), N = number of undecayed nuclei
N = N₀ e^(−λt)
Exponential decay of nuclei
N₀ = initial number of nuclei
A = A₀ e^(−λt)
Decay of activity
A₀ = initial activity
t½ = ln 2 / λ ≈ 0.693 / λ
Half-life
Time for N (or A) to halve

Photons & Quantum Physics

E = hf
Photon energy
E = energy (J), h = 6.63 × 10⁻³⁴ Js, f = frequency (Hz)
E = hc / λ
Photon energy from wavelength
c = 3.00 × 10⁸ ms⁻¹, λ = wavelength (m)
E (eV) = E (J) / 1.60 × 10⁻¹⁹
Converting joules to electronvolts
1 eV = 1.60 × 10⁻¹⁹ J
hf = φ + ½mv²_max
Photoelectric effect equation
φ = work function (J), ½mv²_max = max KE of emitted electron
f₀ = φ / h
Threshold frequency
Minimum frequency needed to eject an electron. Below f₀, no emission regardless of intensity.
eV_s = ½mv²_max
Stopping potential
V_s = stopping potential (V); voltage needed to stop the fastest electrons
λ = h / mv = h / p
de Broglie wavelength
Wave-particle duality. p = momentum (kg ms⁻¹)

Energy Levels & Spectra

ΔE = hf = hc / λ
Photon emitted/absorbed in transition
ΔE = E_higher − E_lower. Emission: photon released. Absorption: photon absorbed.
Line emission spectrum: discrete bright lines. Line absorption: dark lines on continuous background.
Spectra types
Each element has unique spectrum — used for identification
